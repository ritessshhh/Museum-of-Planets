THE/SUN

The sun is a star located at the center of our solar system. It is a massive ball of hot, glowing gases, primarily hydrogen and helium, and it provides the Earth with heat, light, and energy. The sun's immense gravitational pull holds the planets in orbit around it, and its powerful nuclear reactions release vast amounts of energy in the form of sunlight and solar radiation. The sun is about 4.6 billion years old and is expected to continue shining for several billion more years.

THE/MERCURY

Mercury, the closest planet to the sun in our solar system, is a small and rocky world with a diameter of about 4,879 kilometers. Its surface is marked by craters, cliffs, and plains, while its thin atmosphere contains trace amounts of helium and hydrogen. With a slow rotation that takes 59 Earth days and an orbital period of approximately 88 Earth days, Mercury experiences extreme temperature variations, ranging from scorching hot days to frigid cold nights.

THE/VENUS

Venus, often referred to as Earth's "sister planet," is the second planet from the sun in our solar system. Its thick atmosphere, composed predominantly of carbon dioxide, creates a potent greenhouse effect, resulting in surface temperatures that exceed 800 degrees Fahrenheit - hotter than any other planet. Despite its inhospitable environment, Venus has been the target of numerous space missions due to its proximity and similarities to Earth. Its surface is characterized by extensive volcanic plains and it lacks the plate tectonics seen on our home planet. Venus spins in the opposite direction of most planets, and its slow rotation means a Venusian day is longer than its year.

THE/EARTH

Earth, the third planet from the sun in our solar system, is the only known celestial body to support life. Its atmosphere is primarily composed of nitrogen and oxygen, and its unique environmental conditions facilitate a diverse range of ecosystems. The planet features a variety of landscapes, from vast oceans and arid deserts to lush forests and icy poles. Earth is also characterized by its tectonic activity, resulting in mountains, earthquakes, and volcanic eruptions. It takes 24 hours for Earth to complete one rotation on its axis, creating a cycle of day and night, while its 365.25-day journey around the sun defines our year.

THE/MARS

Mars, the fourth planet from the sun in our solar system, is often called the "Red Planet" due to its iron oxide-rich surface that gives it a distinctive reddish appearance. The thin Martian atmosphere, composed largely of carbon dioxide, results in frigid conditions, with average temperatures around -80 degrees Fahrenheit. Despite its harsh environment, Mars has valleys, deserts, polar ice caps, and the tallest volcano in the solar system, Olympus Mons. Scientists have found evidence of past liquid water, leading to ongoing exploration for signs of ancient life. A Martian day, known as a sol, is slightly longer than an Earth day, and its orbit results in a Martian year that is approximately 1.88 Earth years.

THE/JUPITER

Jupiter, the fifth planet from the sun, is the largest planet in our solar system, with a mass over twice that of all the other planets combined. It's a gas giant composed primarily of hydrogen and helium, similar to the sun. Notable for its Great Red Spot, a storm larger than Earth that has been raging for at least 300 years, Jupiter's atmosphere also features distinctive colorful cloud bands. It has a strong magnetic field and dozens of moons, the four largest being the Galilean moons: Io, Europa, Ganymede, and Callisto. Jupiter's day is the shortest of all the planets at just under 10 hours, but its year is quite long, taking about 11.86 Earth years to complete an orbit around the sun.

THE/SATURN

Saturn, the sixth planet from the sun, is a gas giant known for its stunning ring system composed primarily of ice particles with a smaller amount of rocky debris and dust. It's the second-largest planet in our solar system after Jupiter and is primarily made up of hydrogen and helium. The planet's atmospheric patterns include cloud bands and massive storms, similar to Jupiter. Saturn boasts more than 80 moons, the largest of which, Titan, has its own atmosphere and lakes of liquid methane. Saturn has a relatively short day, rotating on its axis every 10.7 hours, but a long year, requiring approximately 29.5 Earth years to complete one orbit around the sun.

THE/URANUS

Uranus, the seventh planet from the sun, is known as an "ice giant" due to its large size and a composition different from the gas giants, with more ices such as water, ammonia, and methane. It has a distinctive pale blue color due to methane in the upper atmosphere absorbing red light. Uranus possesses a unique axial tilt of about 98 degrees, which means it essentially spins on its side, causing extreme seasonal variations. It is surrounded by a system of narrow, faint rings and has 27 known moons named after characters from the works of William Shakespeare and Alexander Pope. Uranus completes a rotation in approximately 17 hours and takes a lengthy 84 Earth years to orbit the sun.
